Why Customer Experience Matters!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 02/06/2016

At the height of Polaroid Camera’s popularity, the company focused more money and time into making photo ink dry faster. But what people really cared about was how their photos were instantly shareable. Kodak, meanwhile, was hard at work at developing the digital camera. The harsh lesson learnt - meet your customers where they are going, not where they are.

Have you ever thought about what might happen if that department store had a coffee shop, or that new coffee shop became an internet café, or sold news papers and magazines, digital gift cards, had a mini post office, became the high streets new digital haven, or even had a place for kids to play inside?

Today, utilities companies are being compared to the very best of retailers – Apple.com / Amazon.com, just for the customer experience they provide: how well they bill their customers, how easily and accurately they monitor your utilities use, and how well integrated their e-commerce channels are with your smart and other internet of thing devices, in your home or office.

So what should you do?

Take initiative in asking for feedback. Provide and create options to see which ones might appeal the most. Remember that about 96% of unhappy customers don’t complain. 91% leave and never come back. Imagine you’re in a relationship with someone and never ask how things are going? Imagine not proactively asking your sports coach for feedback when learning and mastering the technique of a certain sport? You wouldn’t feel embarrassed in asking for it. This is why responding and asking for constructive feedback, with the mindset of constant improvement, is so important.

As part of this process, try to also re-think the questions you might normally ask. If you’re a train company and in a customer survey you ask “Did the train leave on time?”, chances are that they know if it went out on time. Don’t ask questions about data you already have.

Here are some further insights that should help:

When preparing your marketing plans, segment your customers. Knowing who your customers are, and being able to really understand them (their behavior) can be the most difficult, yet most valuable of tasks, when trying to connect emotionally with your customers (especially, the highest use and most loyal users). Sometimes this needs educating them from your side, but you can only start when you have identified who they are.

As mentioned above, leave room for personal choice. In the modern world there is now a huge opportunity to give customers, power, control, and choice. Let them choose their billing cycles, billing date, communications and notifications.

Ask more questions like:

Do we know what our customer journey looks like?

Do we understand where our journey fits in to our customer’s true “end to end” journey?

Have you mapped the customer’s journey in context with “other stuff” a customer does (their purchasing patterns - what, how, and why they buy to fulfill what needs?) during the day. This will help immensely in determining if changes and improvements need to be made.

For example, when a boiler needs replacing, the customer journey doesn’t start when the customer is ready to get a new furnace, but really started when there was a problem with the current furnace. Can you know when this is (what the window of opportunity might be?), or how often this happens? Do certain problems occur or needs arise at only certain times of the year, or after a certain physical events have happened, or conditions have been met? Try and find out.

This journey mapping process, though very useful, is not without its problems, and can usually face several internal obstacles. Hurdles encountered might include getting buy-in from senior management (always very important), getting co-operation from your own staff, getting resources, or just getting started. Various methods can be used to engage these stakeholders, such as internal workshops: to establish buy-in and get senior management to start thinking; internal staff interviews to ascertain if there might be more touch points; or direct customer input through interviews, focus groups and social media.

Doing as such will focus the organization’s thinking on how their services or products are used from an external perspective – identifying the “delight points” and “choke points” of their customers.

As a closing thought, remember that customers are the greatest advocate of a brand, product or service. They’re the ones that tell the stores and make the recommendations. This is why it is always important to engage staff - at all levels - with your organization’s customers AND their experiences of your company and brand.
