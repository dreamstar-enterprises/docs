Data Visualisation and Business Intelligence. What is it? Why is it important? Some Myths debunked!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 04/06/2019

WHAT IS DATA VISUALISATION & BUSINESS INTELLIGENCE?

Data Visualisation and Business Intelligence is the process of aggregating raw data from disparate sources, and then cleaning and transforming this data to build a single and unified data model, so that patterns, associations, and trends, within this data, can be more easily visualised.

Having the right Data Visualisation and Business Intelligence tools in place for an organisation, has the potential to save it significant amounts of time, especially if its data is in a confusing layout, or does not have all the requisite components to perform a complete and meaningful analysis.

In particular, such tools can help…

  • Save significant amounts of time, if data needs to be cleaned, grouped, pivoted, appended, or joined with other sources
  • Robustly automate data workflows, if data resides across several files or folders, that all need to be re-collated and refreshed on a daily basis
  • Display data in Visual Dashboards in a very dynamic way, allowing one to see different views of their data, from the same page

Real life, and practical implementations of Data Visualisation and Business Intelligence solutions can include:

  • Better monitoring and tracking an organisation’s financial performance, covering areas such as the monthly reporting of - Actual, Budgeted, and Forecast - Sales, Profits, Margins, Net Working Capital, and Profit to Sales Conversion Rates – to help uncover both short and long-term trends
  • Providing better insights into an organisation’s reporting of Gender Pay, covering areas such as Staff Head Counts, Average and Median Staff Salaries, Staff Years of Service, and Annual Compensation Ratios, reported by Gender and Department
  • For an investment companies (e.g. Venture Capital firms), providing quicker insights into the financial performance of their portfolio companies to better keep track of measures such as Growth in Unit Volumes, Net Sales, Overheads, and Net or Gross Profit Margins, by Country and Region, across “Actual” and “Budgeted” Scenarios
  • For auto manufacturers, providing a clearer picture into the global sales of all their vehicles, for example: by Trim, Model, Power Train type (Electric or ICE), Rooftop type (Convertible or Non-Convertible), Category type (SUV, Sports, Sedan), etcetera
  • For a Hospital, providing insights into areas such as Length of Stay, No. of In vs. Out Patient visits, Re-Admission Rates, Average Cost per Discharge, Operating Margins, Capital Expenditure, and so on.
  • For a Solar Energy company, helping with giving a clearer picture into areas such as Plant Availability, Mean Time To Repair, Peak Solar Hours, Mean Time Between Failures, No. of Installations by Country, by Region, and so forth

The entire process of “Data Visualisation and Business Intelligence” can be summarised below, as a sequence of four main steps:

Aggregating, and then transforming raw data from several, possibly very dissimilar, data sources, into a set of usable datasets; Then, from these datasets, constructing a single and unified Data Model following a “Fact” and “Dimension” like Table, Data Architecture; and then, from this creating a series of Visual Dashboards, so that the “KPIs” or “Measures” that need to tracked and monitored can be easily done so through graphical means (through types of graphs and charts, and so on)

Note that the area of Data Visualisation & Business Intelligence should not be confused with adjacent areas such as Data Science & Artificial Intelligence. Both are closely allied, but still quite distinct, in terms why they exist, and what they are used for.

For instance, whilst Data Visualisation & Business Intelligence would help an organisation better visualise the different areas it spends its Marketing & Advertising Budget on (for example, by helping to see how much is spent on different advertising mediums, on different vendors, in different countrries), an organisation would instead use Machine Learning Algorithms, if, for example, the Head of its Advertising Department wanted to determine the strength of relationships between its advertising spend and actual sales, to better predict what the quantified lift in sales there might be if it increased its Advertising spend by ‘X’.

WHY IS IT IMPORTANT?

Over the next 5 years, there is predicted to be an increasing need for Data Analytics and its related services from businesses in both the UK and abroad!

The entire field of Data Analytics, Data Science, and Data Visualisation is a rapidly growing one, with many industry participants and observers predicting it will continue to be an area in great need, as many UK companies and businesses try and keep pace with global competition.

Many UK companies, if they haven’t already, are gradually coming to the realisation of just how important fields such as: Data Analytics and Artificial Intelligence, are, not only to their own businesses, but also to improving the nation’s overall health, productivity, and key areas of well-being.

This realisation is also echoed by a recent report by “McKinsey”, the business consulting firm, in its recent article called “Tech for Good”, where it predicts that the technology categories that are likely to have the biggest impact on key areas of well-being, such as Environmental Sustainability, Equal Opportunities, Education, Job Security, and Material Living, will be:

  • Data & Artificial Intelligence
  • Connectivity & Platforms, and
  • Robotics

Furthermore, amongst these 3 categories, it is Data & Artificial Intelligence, (which includes areas such as Advanced Analytics), that they predict is likely to have the biggest single impact.

The increasing need for such services is also reflected by recent research, carried out by “The Department for International Trade”. The UK trade department predicts that “the field of ‘Data Analytics’ is predicted to grow by 177% over the next 5 years”, reinforcing the much broader and deeper economic trends underway in the UK economy: that as more and more UK companies become digital, the more they are looking for external support to improve their data gathering, storage, analytical, and data visualisation capabilities.

This need to digitise, if for no other reason, is being driven by a strong economic imperative to keep up with global competition, particularly that coming from countries such as the United States, and China, and indeed many other countries, which are already far ahead, or quickly catching up, in many economically and technologically important areas such as: Renewable Energy, Artificial Intelligence, Healthcare, Electric Mobility, and Telecommunications, to name but a few!

It is therefore likely that there will only continue to be a need, by many UK businesses, both large and small, for external support in the areas of Data Analytics and Visualisation, at least for the next 5 years, and quite possibly, several years thereafter!
