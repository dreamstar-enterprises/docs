The pace of Macro-structural development - should you care?

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 19/06/2017

Here is an interesting question:

For most of our species existence, our civilization’s macro-structural development has been much slower than it is now. For example, a millennium ago, an entire millennium might have elapsed without a single significant technological invention, and without any noticeable increase in human knowledge and understanding.

So, if you came across a magic lever that could allow you to change the rate of our macro-structural development (solar and fusion technology, artificial intelligence, advanced robotics, autonomous cars, genomics, etc.) what might you do? Accelerate it, decelerate it, or leave things as they are?

The answer to this question (as I have found out from the book Super Intelligence - by Nick Bostrom) requires you to consider the effects of existential risk. To understand this, you can think about two very distinct kinds of risks: “state risks” and “step risks”.

A state risk is associated with being in a certain state – with the risk being a direct function of how long the system remains in that state.

Risks from nature are typically (not always, but typically) state risks: The longer we remain exposed, the greater our chance of being destroyed by an asteroid, supervolcanic eruption, gamma ray burst, naturally arising pandemic, etc..

A step risk by contrast is a discrete risk associated with some necessary or desirable transition. Once the transition is complete, the risk vanishes.

The amount of step risk associated with a transition is usually not a simple function of how long a transition takes place. One doesn’t halve the risk of traversing a minefield by simply running twice as fast.

For example, the creation of general super intelligence might be a step risk. There may be a risk associated with takeoff, the magnitude of which would depend on the preparations that have been made. The amount of risk might not depend on whether the takeoff takes twenty milliseconds or twenty hours.

The upshot of all this is that changes in the pace of macro-structural development become important because they affect how well we (humanity) are prepared for when the time comes to confront any possible step risks.

If the challenge confronted were to solve a problem from which learning from experience was key, then the chronological length of the preparation period is most likely to be the key determining factor, since time is needed for the requisite experience to accumulate.

If, however, the problem called for foresight, reasoning and theoretical insight (as might be the case with artificial intelligence technology), it is much less clear how historical experience or any historical precedence would help. What might instead matter is the amount of skill and intelligence to implement the “best available” solution at that time.

Consider too, for example, the following argument template for proceeding with research for what might be considered to be technology X with some benefits but also some risks:

  1. The risks of X are great

  2. Reducing these risks will require a period of serious preparation

  3. Serious preparation will begin only once the prospect of X is taken seriously by broader society

  4. Broad sectors of society will only take X seriously only once a large research effort to develop X is underway

  5. The earlier a research effort is initiated, the longer it will take to deliver X

  6. Therefore, the earlier a research effort is initiated, the longer the period during which serious preparation can happen, and the greater the reduction of the risks

  7. Therefore, a serious research effort toward X should be initiated immediately

Thus what ends up looking like a reason for slowing down ends up becoming a compelling reason to speed up!

A similar argument goes with why small scale technological set backs can actually be a good thing and nothing to be disheartened about. They make you more aware of its vulnerabilities and can spur you into taking the next necessary actions and precautions. This is indeed very similar to how your body’s natural defence system might sometimes need a relatively small inoculation for curable diseases to deal with more serious forms of the disease.

So does this make you a believer in a healthy pace of macro-structural development?

It certainly does for me! It means we have a greater chance of experiencing a more technologically advanced future, and all the benefits that go with it! So, the pace of Macro-structural development - should you care? Definitely!
