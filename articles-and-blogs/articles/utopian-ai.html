The Utopian View of A.I.

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 30/03/2019

Why should we promote and develop human-entered A.I. technologies and applications? In fact, why should we as humanity, really care about A.I. at all? These were questions that came to mind when reading an article recently by Reid Hoffman: Entrepreneur, Product Strategist, and Investor, and avid advocate of business entrepreneurship.

Reid writes a wonderfully inspirational article that describes how businesses and governments should not just take a dystopian view of “Artificial Intelligence”, reflecting on the things that could possibly go wrong with “A.I.”, but also tries to see the positive, and transformational impacts that “A.I.” could have on society at large.

He points out that “A.I.”, as we witness it today, holds the same potential to positively tranform society today, (increasing both human productivity gains and prosperity), just as the Industrial Revolutions did in the 17th and 18th centuries:

For example,

  • Railroad and telegraphs, during the First Industrial Revolution, created new networks of communication and distribution.

  • Electrification, during the Second Industrial Revolution, meant that people could do more at night than they could before.

  • Larger buildings, and now Sky Scrapers have now also allowed companies to pursue increasingly sophisticated endeavours.

These new technologies and developments haven’t just changed how people, goods, and information get from one place to another, but more importantly, have changed the way people can dream about the future! How they can expand their conceptions of what individuals can aspire to, but also how they achieve greater meaning and purpose in their lives.

I believe, just as Reid Hoffman does, that “Artificial Intelligence”, and other similar technologies like it, have the potential to re-shape and transform the societies we live and work in, as much as (if not, more than) the discoveries and breakthroughs did of the First and Second Industrial Revolutions.

And though I can understand why some people might hold negative views about “A.I.”, believing that corporations, (particularly those at the forefront of “A.I.”), will always favour “A.I.” and its own corporate profits, over something that might actually be better for humanity and the planet – such people should not always believe things as so.

Yes, government oversight is important when creating productive, prosperous, and well-managed societies. But, well-connected, global organisations, inventors, business entrepreneurs, and a myriad of other start-ups, help to create social progress too!

And history tells us this! As Reid Hoffman quite clearly points out, the gains the world has made since the First Industrial Revolution, which began in England, in the mid-1700s, are so astounding that it is all too easy to lose sight of them!

Humans now live more than twice as long as most people did then — and we can also make a 500-mile trip in roughly 1/100th of the time it would have taken in 1750.

And though governments have played an important role in this - by providing social stability and key physical infrastructure - ‘breakthrough innovations’ like the steam engine, standardized parts, and the factory assembly line didn’t arise out of official government programs or initiatives. They were the work of inventors, business entrepreneurs, and corporations - in search of profits to sustain their efforts!

… So when it comes to the question of whether we should promote and develop human-entered A.I. technologies and applications, or, care about A.I. at all, I think we really should!

When it comes to A.I., it is of course important to ask the question, “What could possibly go wrong?”. New technologies have created new challenges like pollution, and climate change – and the world still faces the kinds of inequity, conflict, and injustice, that plagued humanity in the past.

But it is also important to ask the question, “What could go right!”

For example,

  • How might we see “A.I.” as a catalyst to enable progress in the industry you work in?

  • What might the utopian view of “A.I.” look like for your industry?

  • How might A.I. seamlessly be woven into it?

  • How might A.I. be used create educational tools and services that make it possible for people in your industry to maximize their potential?

  • How might new forms of emotionally attuned robot companions abolish or at least greatly diminish human loneliness, in your industry?

As Reid points out, A.I. won’t be the magic answer to all humanity’s problems — but its important to also see its utopian view of A.I.. And this is one that will only be as magical as people who have the self-belief, (and resources, network, and innate ability!) dream it to be!
