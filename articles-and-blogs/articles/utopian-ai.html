The Utopian View of A.I.

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 30/03/2019

Why should we promote and develop human-entered A.I. technologies and applications? In fact, why should we as humanity, really care about A.I. at all? These were questions that came to mind when reading an article recently by Reid Hoffman: Entrepreneur, Product Strategist, and Investor, and avid advocate of business entrepreneurship.

Reid writes a wonderfully inspirational article that describes how businesses and governments should not just take a dystopian view of “Artificial Intelligence”, reflecting on the things that could possibly go wrong with “A.I.”, but also tries to see the positive, and transformational impacts that “A.I.” could have on society at large.

He points out that “A.I.”, as we witness it today, holds the same potential to positively tranform society today, (increasing both human productivity gains and prosperity), just as the Industrial Revolutions did in the 17th and 18th centuries:

For example,

  • Railroad and telegraphs, during the First Industrial Revolution, created new networks of communication and distribution.

  • Electrification, during the Second Industrial Revolution, meant that people could do more at night than they could before.

  • Larger buildings, and now Sky Scrapers have now also allowed companies to pursue increasingly sophisticated endeavours.

These new technologies and developments haven’t just changed how people, goods, and information get from one place to another, but more importantly, have changed the way people can dream about the future! How they can expand their conceptions of what individuals can aspire to, but also how they achieve greater meaning and purpose in their lives.

I believe, just as Reid Hoffman does, that “A.I.” (and other similar technologies that get labelled under the heading A.I.), have the potential to have the same kinds of economic and cultural impacts on society, as the discoveries and breakthroughs of the First and Second Industrial Revolution.

Whilst I can understand why people may view “A.I.” very negatively, believing that, because corporations always appear to be at the forefront of “A.I.” efforts and developments, that they will always favour “A.I.”, and its profits, over whatever works best for humanity – we should not always see things as so.

Yes, government oversight is important for creating productive, prosperous, and well managed societies, but, well-connected global organisations, inventors, business entrepreneurs, and a myriad of other start-ups help create massive social progress too!

And history tells us this! As Reid quite clearly points out, the gains the world has made since the First Industrial Revolution, which began in England, in the mid-1700s, are so astounding that it’s all too easy to lose sight of them!

Humans now live more than twice as long as most people did then — and yet we can also make a 500-mile trip in roughly 1/100th of the time it would have taken in 1750.

And though governments have played an important role in this - by providing social stability and key physical infrastructure - breakthrough world innovations like the steam engine, standardized parts, and the factory assembly line didn’t arise out of official government programs or initiatives. They were the work of inventors, business entrepreneurs, and corporations - in search of profits to sustain their efforts!

… So when it comes to the question of whether we should promote and develop human-entered A.I. technologies and applications, or, care about A.I. at all, I think we really should!

When it comes to A.I., it is of course important to ask, “What could possibly go wrong?”. New technologies have created new challenges like pollution, and climate change – and the world still faces the kinds of inequity, conflict, and injustice, that plagued humanity in the past.

But it is also important to ask “What could go right!”

For example,

  • How might we see “A.I.” as a catalyst to enable progress in the industry you work in?

  • What might the utopian view of “A.I.” look like for your industry?

  • How might A.I. seamlessly be woven into it?

  • How might A.I. be used create educational tools and services that make it possible for people in your industry to maximize their potential?

  • How might new forms of emotionally attuned robot companions abolish or at least greatly diminish human loneliness, in your industry?

As Reid points out, A.I. won’t be the magic answer to all humanity’s problems — but its important to also see its utopian view of A.I.. And this is one that will only be as magic as people have the self-belief, (and resources, network, and innate ability!) to dream it to be!
