What’s happening to Global Trade?

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 07/04/2016

The international movement of goods, services and money has been a defining feature of globalization and an increasingly connected world. Over the past quarter of a century the value of global trade (in goods, services, flows of money) constituted to about 25% of GDP. Today, its now almost 40%. In 1990, there were 400m foreign trips abroad (business travelers, tourists and students). Today this has grown to 1.1bn.

Most people are aware of the benefits of globalization: it helps companies recruit for the best talent, wherever it may be, and also increases competition among companies, keeping them on their toes, causing them to be more efficient, and to innovate new products and services, which in turn drives economic growth.

What is interesting though is how the mix in international trade has changed over the past few years. Global trade of goods is now thought to be flat. The global trade of data however has exploded - up by 45 times in less than 10 years! Since the financial crises the trade of goods has never really recovered, and when measured against the size of the world economy, has actually slowed down.

Now we see that a lot of activities linked to data are increasingly linked to trade of services and the exchange of ideas and innovation. It is estimated that 10% of global goods trade is made of e-commerce, and for B2B, this is 25%. In fact, 50% of world trade in services is now digital.

All these changes have resulted in the rapid democratization of the global economy. When the economy used to be driven by a select few multinational corporations, digital platforms have enabled businesses around the world, even start-ups to participate. For example, Alibaba in China now has 10 million small to medium sized enterprises that sell products through its platform. Amazon’s platform gives all its customers both scale and reach. Estimates now put it that up to 86% of technology start-ups create and get their business in the global market, from customers outside their home country.

While all of this is exciting, especially for a technology buff like me, it is still important to remember that there are only a few countries that are really highly connected to the global economy. The (marginal) benefit of cross-border data flows is much greater for emerging economies, or countries on the periphery.

With all this happening, Digital literacy skills everywhere have also become important, and created a need to have a good digital infrastructure so people can participate in the digital economy. Many fear that if they don’t, they risk falling behind. Singapore, for example, despite being a small country with few natural resources tops the MGI Global Connectedness Index - a consequence of its very deliberate decision to become a regional hub in the trade of goods, and business service hub, in South East Asia.

The alarming statistic now is that the world economy is $10 trillion smaller than it could be if there were greater digital connectedness between countries. Another is that where trade might start to go down by a factor of 2 for every 100km away from where you stand, with digital flows, the elasticity of trade has almost been cut in half.

The future is therefore bright, and if companies consider using the right digital platforms, the payoffs could be tremendous. It starts, and needs to start, however, from being digital in the first place!
