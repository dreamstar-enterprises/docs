The A.I. Playbook (how to get started!)

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 18/10/2018

Artificial intelligence (A.I.) is clearly destined to become one of the greatest technological advances of our time, but as the technology rapidly develops, how can companies directly benefit from applying A.I. to their current processes and operations?

For companies, practical A.I. applications can manifest in all sorts of ways depending on the organizational needs and insights derived from any data that is collected. Applications include everything from mining social data to driving engagement in customer relationship management (CRM), to optimizing logistics and improving efficiency when tracking and managing assets.

Companies, however, should not rush in on the A.I. bandwagon just for the sake of having A.I.! Rather, a clear implementation framework will be needed to avoid ad hoc approaches that: fail to scale; cannot prove out new technologies; or just outright fall flat in building out the right system capabilities – all, ultimately, resulting in large wasted efforts!

To help set you on the right path (and, thus, avoid approaches that are just doomed to fail!), here are some steps businesses can take to more successfully integrate A.I. into their organizations!

Identify the specific problems you want A.I. to solve!

Start by thinking about how you can add A.I. capabilities to your existing products and services. Do you have in mind specific use cases in which A.I. could solve business problems or provide demonstrable value? Try and move quickly from big-picture concepts to concrete and specific applications. Explore these applications further by talking to venture capitalists, research labs, your own IT department, or through internal brainstorming.

Acknowledge any internal capability gaps!

Identify the barriers to A.I. application in your company. Is it lack of required talent and capabilities, maturity of the A.I. technology, management being unclear about A.I.’s value, difficult in identifying business use cases, lack of regulatory support, data availability, or insufficient computing infrastructure.

Be clear and be honest about any gaps or barriers. For example, an A.I. application focused on automated product ideation might experience feasibility gaps with regard to data (for instance, a lack of quality information or diverse data sets). An algorithm could lack the sophistication to deliver needed decision-making insights.

What are you seeking? Operational enhancement or Product innovation?

Try and be clear also on your end goal. Do your strive for greater “efficiency” & “effectiveness”, or a “better” product / service?

Operational enhancements could cover research, product development, manufacturing, supply-chain management, or marketing and sales (e.g. (i) introducing A.I.-enhanced predictive maintenance in manufacturing, (ii) developing R&D applications that could recommend modification areas to drive productivity)

Product innovations might include automated driver-assistance system modules

Accurately assess the level of potential disruption!

Next, try to assess and prioritize the potential business and financial value (e.g. ROI) the various A.I. solutions or initiatives you’ve identified could bring. For example: by evaluating each across the dimensions of “potential” and “feasibility”.

Don’t forget to ask both product and value-chain questions, at this stage.

Product questions might include:

  • Does it provide an efficiency boost or cost reduction? (E.g. deep-learning models capable of making trade-offs / optimisations across cost, production limitations, and customers’ preferences – potentially reducing design cycles and lowering labor costs
  • Can it enable product substitution, affecting quantities of current products sold (and increasing others)?
  • Does it integrate the value chain by combining segments?
  • Will it speed up product commoditization, and thus affect product price and margin?
  • Will it enable a better allocate resources (e.g. optimisation of staff allocation models)?

Value-chain questions might include:

  • Does it provide alternative monetization models that can generate additional revenue from existing customers?
  • Can it change the value chain from a linear structure to an ecosystem or a platform?

The more you can answer these questions with a “yes,” the larger the likely disruption should become.

With the costs and benefits now clearly evaluated, start to build in ownership and get recognition from top-level executives. Buy-in, here, gets you to the next stage!

Bring in Experts and set up a Pilot Project

When ready, organizationally and technologically, start building and integrating. Start small, have project goals in mind, and, be aware of what you know and what you don’t know about A.I.!

Unless you can’t for tactical reasons, bring in outside A.I. experts and specialists. Getting internal and external people together in a small team, maybe 4-5 people, within a short timeframe of 2-3 months, ought to keep the whole team focused on more straightforward (and also more achievable) goals!

Establish an “A.I. war room”, if necessary, to bring people together to share knowledge rapidly and promote iterative working styles.

After the pilot is completed, decide on a longer-term, more elaborate project if the value proposition makes sense for your business!

Establish Governance around your new model

To get governance right, here, ask questions such as: What will your new target organizational model for A.I. look like? Will an “A.I. engine” be needed to provide support across the businesses? Can you set up agile ways of working, potentially establishing an A.I. council to make strategic decisions?

Also, since we’re talking about governance, what data-quality principles, policies, and practices that reflect AI considerations might you establish? Also, how might you build a clear “playbook” of methodologies to consolidate cross-functional learnings?

Infrastructure: Architect with balance!

Governance and Infrastructure design are closely interlinked, but your goal at the end of this stage is to obtain high-quality data, integrate data siloes together, and sort out inconsistencies so that the data is accurate and rich, with all the right dimensions required for A.I.!

Focus on the following areas:

  • Re-designing work flows appropriately, to reflect the new operating model.
  • Cleaning all data to make it ready to avoid a “garbage in, garbage out” scenario
  • Integrating data-management solutions with the existing enterprise architecture (i.e. work to overcome siloes of different legacy systems!)
  • Deciding on final storage requirements to implement your A.I. solution - consolidating redundant data-warehouse assets, where what’s remaining will require close oversight and the effective allocation of resources
  • The final nuts and bolts of A.I., including the establishment of data architecture with data lakes and data-search layers.

Remember that storage solutions need to be fast and optimised, as without huge volumes of data to help build more accurate models, A.I. systems may improve enough to effectively achieve its objectives

Finally, Architect with balance! Consider both the research / business goals of A.I., alongside the software / hardware limitations you are likely to face. In other words, ensure you build in sufficient bandwidth for storage, the graphics processing unit (GPU), and networking, for the resources you have. Consider too, building in flexibility to allow for the repurposing of hardware as user requirements change!

Build world-class Talent!

Decide on what people you need to build needed A.I. capabilities and competencies. What data scientists, analytics engineers, translators, or algorithms will you need? Can you compliment this with a multiyear plan to attract and retain the right A.I. talent? Do you have a clear talent plan (identify, recruit, develop, and retain) in place? Remember, if attracting talent is hard, retaining it is often more challenging!

A.I. talent requires more attention than talent in other areas. For example, CEOs (and other top CXOs) need to be personally involved too, to show how serious the company is about A.I. and the individual.

Involve both the business units and top leadership in these discussions since the entire organization needs to understand the new roles and responsibilities that an A.I. transformation will necessitate.

Create the right Culture!

Finally, foster a culture and understand the best working environment to achieve success!

It’s not easy, trust me! (I speak from having experienced many bad company cultures). Here, fashioning an A.I.-focused culture can be the toughest part of a transformation. The key will often be in being able to bridge the gap between the technical sphere (how do we make A.I. work?) and the business world (which real-life problems can it solve?).

Perhaps, to begin with, nurture a start-up-like environment to support capability building and to boost motivation.

Closing remarks…

The coming A.I. surge is the seventh of the great technological waves over the past 50 years, and, as many business practioners and technologists predict, is likely to fundamentally redefine 21st-century business practices. While potentially overestimated in the near term, its long-term impact is likely to be profound, and why top company leaders need to get some skin in the game! This means investing despite currently high levels of uncertainty, otherwise those who wait may end up being just caught out!
