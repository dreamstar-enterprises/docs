A.I. on our work and lives!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 12/07/2017

Viewpoints on how Mike Quindazzi believes A.I. is likely to affect our work and lives (the way we work, transact, commute, and socialise).

It’s a continuum

Firstly, recognise that A.I. means different things to different people, and its best to think of successful developments in the technology along more of a continuum rather than just one definitive final breakthrough – there are different levels of A.I.: from assisted, augmented, to fully autonomous.

Focusing on Assisted AI, what does this mean?

Assisted means things such as email filtering, and automating a process (e.g. insurance claims)

How might A.I. affect our jobs?

Most studies are pessimistic. One forecast study by PwC (a sample of one!) suggested between 20 to 30% of jobs could be at risk of being taken over by A.I. by 2030, with the potential for automation highest in transport (56%), followed by manufacturing (46%)

Are we all doomed? Is it as simple as this?

No, not really… for various economic, legal, and regulatory reasons not all jobs will be automated.

Importantly, the true economic benefit comes from the productivity gains that A.I. is likely to bring. This will create the extra wealth that should result in extra spending that should in turn support the creation of more service jobs.

The overall impact will depend though on how the workforce evolves, the social acceptance towards it, and the pace of actual technology advancement (i.e. how hard people work towards developing it).

Also, though total (pre-tax) incomes may rise, it may not be equally spread across all income groups of society.

What will AI actually do?

A.I. will start to replace certain human cognitive work, as devices and machines with sensors operate according to advance machine learning algorithms. This will initially be at handling very repetitive tasks (in more predictable and stable environments)

A.I. will also be used to help analyse and extract patterns from enormous sets of data (which only seems to be on an upward trend - accelerated by phenomena such as IoT, and continued development and use of APIs).

What should workers do?

Not panic! Though surveys do predict some job losses – people’s views are likely to have already been heavily influenced by how much A.I. has been trending in social media, and the mainstream media. There will also always be concerns around privacy, trust and regulations that will have to be overcome.

What should businesses do?

Well, most CEOs actually plan to increase hiring (52% according to a recent PwC Survey). Instead, they worry about a shortage of workers with soft skills – creative leaders with high emotional intelligence

Given such a global push to developing A.I. though, all businesses will need to:

a) Plan for an AI enabled world, and
b) Better understand how they can get value from it
