Arrival of the Robots

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 07/04/2016

According to many economists (and university professors), many of the jobs that exist today are at a high risk of computerization and will either not exist, or change in a very significant way over the next two decades.

As a consequence people are wondering how education could be used to insulate the next generation of workers from such a fate. In the last wave of automation policymakers reasoned that if machines were going to substitute for brawn, more people would need to use their brain. Things had become a race between education and technology, and humans won that race! Now the race has become technology, against both brains and brawn!

Smart Machines have long since surpassed humans in their ability to read and do arithmetic, but are catching up with writing (where humans still have the comparative advantage).

So what does this mean and why is it important?

Well, still being creative is a virtue. Artificial Intelligence (AI for short) tends to solve problems methodically, and cannot make the same intuitive logical leaps of imagination that humans can, such as how to cook a delicious meal, or write a poem! Can a robot compose music or direct a film?

Some machines have attempted to learn how to seem caring, but humans still have an unsurpassed ability for tolerance and empathy and why jobs of the future are likely to involve more EQ than IQ.

So what does the next generation of workers think?

Well, in an interview with 8,700 youngsters, when asked what they felt would be the most significant drivers of change, they said:

  • Mobile Internet & Cloud Technology
  • Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning
  • The rise of Big Data
  • The Internet of Things
  • Advanced Robotics and Autonomous Transport
  • Advanced Manufacturing and 3D printing

These are trends that are important to be aware of, and if you observe trends in the news over the past 5 years, are affecting all data intensive industries from financial services (banking etc,), retail, manufacturing and science in a very real way.

Though all is not at loss. Even if a more ubiquitous age of artificial intelligence does arrive, cognitive skills such as reading, writing and maths will still be needed. Why? Because they create the mental frameworks that allow us to solve higher order problems creatively. And just like in the last technological revolution, we are all likely to adapt and change our future roles to our future jobs.
