The UK Film & Entertainment Industry

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 07/04/2016

Who are the key players of the UK film industry?

The BFI is the lead organization for film in the UK. It was founded in 1933, and is governed by Royal Charter. It is considered a Government arm’s-length body and distributor of Lottery funds for film to enable it to widen its strategic focus.

The organization has a cultural, creative and industrial role, and brings together activities such as the BFI National Archive, Cultural Programmes, Publishing, and Festivals for film production and education.

The (Pinewood) Studio Group is the leading provider of studio and related services to the film and television industry. They have an impressive heritage that dates back to the 20th Century.

They have the best quality sound stages, a unique underwater filming stage, plenty of state of the art TV studios, and digital content services.

The studios give access to very experienced crews, and the expertise of hundreds of other complimentary media companies located at its Pinewood and Shepperton Media Hubs.

The British Film Commission (BFC) is a national body that supports the production of international feature films, and high end television.

It has plenty of industry sponsors, such as Disney, Warner Bros UK, BBC Worldwide, Elstree studios, and Pinewood studios.

It is managed by Film London, through a public and private partnership with commercial sponsors, and funding from the Department of Culture, Media and Sports, the BFI, and UK Trade and Investment.

The UKIE is a trade body that supports, grows and promotes the UK game’s and interactive entertainment industry. It work’s with the government and the media to raise the awareness of the industry’s positive economic contribution and societal benefits (as read by policy makers, regulators, and consumers).

PACT is a trade association that represents the commercial interests of 500 independent TV, feature film and digital media companies.

It supports its members with legal and business affairs advice and support, providing a range of services, along with campaign work.

Value of the Industry

The UK film and media sector is a dynamic, innovative, internationally respected and growing sector!

All aspects of the industry, from British films, video games, animation and television, are enjoyed by audiences all over the world.

The sector brings cultural value and real economic benefit, adding billions to GDP and jobs.

Its growth also benefits other parts of the economy, empowering its workforce, to develop new skills.

The sector’s creative sector tax reliefs have provided much success too, with total production expenditure by films that claim tax relief standing at £7.8bn (72% of which is spent here in the UK).

Many prominent figures also say it is the golden sector for the UK, and will continue to lead many people in fields of innovation, creativity and art.

A few key facts!

Film

The sector has supported (as latest figures would suggest)

  • 39,800 FTE of direct employment
  • created £1.4bn in direct Gross Value Added (GVA)

It is a sector with one of the highest export intensity’s, with exports accounting for 65% of sectoral GVA.

Private sector capital investments of more than £425 million have been made since 2007, including organizations in the South East - Pinewood Shepperton plc, Warner Bros, Industrial Light and Magic to those in the South West such as Bristol.

Of the £21bn spent on tourism in the UK in 2013, £840 million can be attributed to film-induced tourism. This:

  • supports 8,400 FTEs of employment
  • generates £400 million in GVA
  • contributes £92 million to the government

UK made films also led to an estimated £226 million in merchandise sales in 2013

For each pound of Film Tax Relief (FTR) from 2006-07, to 2013-14, £12.49 was created through direct and multiplier effects.

High End TV

In 2013, the High End Television (HETV) sector:

  • supported 8,300 FTEs
  • generated £382m in direct GVA

Taking into account the total economic consideration (including multiplier and spillover effects) the UK HETV sector in 2013:

  • supported 16,800 FTEs
  • generated £852 million in GVA
  • contributed £249 million to the government

Video Games

In 2013, the core UK video games sector

  • supported 12,100 FTEs in direct employment
  • contributed £755 million in direct GVA

For the core UK video games sector (UK made video games) in 2013, it

  • supported 23,900 FTEs of employment
  • generated £1.4 bn in GVA
  • contributed £429 million to the Exchequer

Animation Programmes

In 2013, the animation sector

  • supported 1,300 FTEs of direct employment
  • contributed £54 million in direct GVA

Taking into account multiplier affects, this sector:

  • supported 4,700 FTEs of employment
  • generated £171 million in GVA
  • contributed £52 million to the Exchequer

Compared to other television genres, animation producers create a much larger share of revenue and economic returns from downstream programme sales and licensing activities, and why they tend to produce higher ancillary revenues

Final remarks

The sector is likely to continue to contribute substantially to the UK’s cultural life, with a rich diversity of stories and talent working as important cultural signposts domestically and overseas.

There are many natural synergies too between HETV and film: many cast and crew work across both, and the technical aspects of productions often match film more closely than traditional TV.

And just as with film, High End TV is also proving a hit, with successful productions such as 24-24, Live Another Day, and Outlander, shot in Scotland, Galavant, in Bristol, Da Vinci Demons, in Swansea, and Game of Thrones, which continues to be shot in Belfast.
