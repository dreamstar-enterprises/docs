Experiences to date - UofCambridge, PwC, Manchester Business School, Jaguar Land Rover, WPP, and Diageo!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 09/12/2019

A big thanks to Harsh Batra (Vice President of Ethos Data) for arranging this! And also to all the other great people - colleagues, coaches, mentors, and friends - I’ve had the opportunity to work with so far in my career!

Sachin Tandon is one of the smartest and humblest guys I have ever known. In this wide ranging conversation, we go back in time, to unravel his career journey to date - how, and why, he ended up studying and working at the places he did (at some very well-known institutions across the world), and then after some years working as a company employee, took the big leap into independent freelancing, to see if that is where he could work on problems and coming up with great solutions for the companies and industries he was most passionate about.

I personally see him as a collector of experiences. Someone with a strong foundation of skills - that takes decades to acquire - but underlying it all, a childish curiosity to want to keep learning and keep growing - with humility, focus, and gratitude at its very core.

The skill sets, and dimension of understanding he brings to businesses, in his freelancing capacity, is something very unique to the business universe! If you get the opportunity to work with him, I’d say you, and your business, are very lucky!

You can read all about him, in this interview below. It’s quite lengthy, but very comprehensive read, and definitely all worth it!

Introduction

Harsh Batra - (VP of Ethos Data)

In this one to one, Sachin Tandon talks through some of his career highlights to date: from his early experiences in Business Consulting, Assurance, and Finance at PwC, in London, to his then later endevours with International Commerce at Manchester Business School, to then some of his more recent entrepreneurial stints, working with companies such as Jaguar Land Rover, WPP, Diageo, the Omnicom Media Group.

Sachin currently freelances through his own small company, with the mission of wanting to “to support businesses around the world – whether they be small and innovative start-ups, medium-sized companies, or large multi-nationals – to achieve more!” – He particularly focuses on those companies in the Technology & Engineering, and Media & Entertainment sectors as ones that interest him the most.

He has had quite “non-linear” career path to date. After completing a degree in General Engineering at the University of Cambridge, U.K., and then M.I.T., Boston, U.S., he began his graduate career at the Professional Services firm, PriceWaterhouseCoopers (PwC), in London. After a few years here, he did his MBA from Manchester Business School, before then taking the very “entrepreneurial leap”, of turning his home into his own start-up / freelancing headquarters, to pursue what he felt like, was a small gap in the market, and also growing area of interest for him.

What follows is a transcript of my conversation with Sachin, where along with some his career highlights, he shares some important life lessons, and tells us how it’s all going, and what he hopes to do next.

Sachin thanks for taking the time to do this!

Hello Harsh! Thanks for having me!

So, Sachin, just looking at your career history to date, you’ve definitely gone on and done a lot of different things - more than I might possibly say of most – but it’s clear to me at least, that you have taken a strong liking to business. As a younger boy, when you were growing up, did you always have an ambition to get into business? Was it something always on the radar when you were coming through the schooling system you grew up in?

No, not really. When I was young, I was always quite focused on my schoolwork. Outside school, in my free time, I used to watch a lot of films by Stephen Spielberg, and read books by H.G. Wells, Jules Verne, Isaak Asimov, and Arthur C. Clarke. I thought one day, I wanted to learn more about what Film Directors did, or some create whacky flying gadget, or flying machine, and become some kind of Engineering Rocket Scientist. But then also, after watching TV series like Ally McBeal, and Due South, I thought I might want to become some kind of Lawyer, or be a Mounty Police Officer, with a Siberian Husky, called Diefenbaker.

Then having become fascinated with Technology, and by what Engineers do, when I was 18 - I did my Gap Year, and many of my University internships with Engineering companies, carrying out research and conducting experiments in dedicated Engineering research labs, like what say, many Engineering Rocket Scientists might do. Then after 5 years of that, I decided I wanted to still do innovative and creative things, but in other ways too, from new perspectives, and explore how this could be done from other business functions too - which is how I found myself wanting to learn more about areas such as Finance, Accounting, and Tax, and then after that, in areas such as Marketing, and Human Resources, and so on, from when I did my MBA - again just to understand how I could best help other people – which is what I think a successful career in business is really all about!

That’s great. You’ve achieved quite a lot in your career so far – and I see gone on to some pretty impressive places, and done work equally impressive. What, in your own mind, do you think you would put your success down to?

Well, if I had to name just one thing, it would be tenacity!

Throughout my career, I’ve recognised the importance of dealing with both success and failure. And where I’ve met with success, I’ve tried to build on those successes, but just as importantly, whenever I’ve failed, I’ve always tried to ensure I’ve learnt from these experiences – so I can “bounce back”, learn from them, and have another go!

That tenacity (or perseverance, if you will), along with the ability to communicate clearly, with empathy, and form strong relationships with people, I would say have been the critical success factors to any life and work success I think I’ve had so far.

I remember once, in a work interview, I was once asked: what is the one trait I would most like to be remembered for. And, at the time I can remember saying: “Just never giving up”. I think that one trait has and always be something I will carry with me.

Thanks Sachin! Do you think it is these qualities that have allowed you to get on well with the people you have worked with so far?

I think it was one of the really important things, for definite.

When I think about the people I really admire and look up to, we often both have a number of things in common, but also a number of things that are different too. But that idea of “Just never giving up”, and always “Striving for excellence” are qualities we both have often shared.

University - Engineering

I see that after your primary and secondary schooling, you went to University, and did a Master’s Degree in Manufacturing Engineering. After school, why did you decide to do that and pursue Higher Education? How was it?

For me, I just saw going to University as a natural next step from the Further Education and Schooling System I had grown up in. I had worked hard (put in a lot of time, energy, and effort) into my “A-level” subjects, and then early on in my second year at Sixth Form College, knew that I was also on track to getting high grades in the subjects I had taken.

At the time, my thinking was that I wanted to follow in my Father’s and Grandfather’s footsteps and pursue my Higher Education in Engineering. Both my Dad and Grandfather had studied Engineering when they went to college. Both had worked as Engineers too early on in their careers.

My Grandfather was a Civil Engineer and used to design and build Hydro-Electric dams in India in the 1970s and 1980s, because he aspired to reduce the country’s reliance on Fossil Duels.

My Father started his career working as a Chemical Engineer for companies here in the U.K. like Dunlop, and Slazenger, designing innovative Tennis balls and Tennis racquets – Racquets that were made from lighter composite materials, and Tennis Balls filled with more complex gases that made them more durable, last longer and bounce better. After Dunlop, he then worked for some years at companies such as: Bowater, Rexam, and Bonnar, to help them design and make polythene rolls – those used for wrapping the Bales of Hay you see dotted around the English countryside, before then moving onto a more International career in General Management.

So, aspiring to follow a similar path, I thought I too would want to go to a well-known University, to read Engineering - one that was well-resourced, had a good facilities, and admitted a lot of motivated, smart, and ambitious students, who I could learn a lot from.

After doing some research, I saw that Cambridge was highly ranked in the University League Tables, and that both its Engineering course, and Undergraduate Teaching had a strong global reputation. I was also left with a positive impression after visiting them on an Open-Day. So, I decided to try my luck and apply. Then, after a couple of interviews, I was lucky enough to be accepted onto their 4 Year Masters of Engineering programme, at the college, Gonville & Caius.

As to how it was, overall, I had a really enjoyable time. My college, Gonville & Caius was one of the smaller ones – but still very idyllic, and picturesque. It was conveniently located close to the City Centre, where all the shops were, and wasn’t very far either from the University’s main Engineering department.

The college itself had a very nice, close-knit, community feel to it. Our supervisors and professors were really wonderful people - very passionate about teaching, knowledgable about the subjects they taught, and about the well-being of the students. Gonville & Caius college itself had a very diverse mix of International students, with students that read not just Engineering, but subjects ranging all the way from: Medicine, Mathematics, Natural Sciences, Languages, Music, Economics, Philosophy, Computer Science, to the Classics, and English Literature.

It even had, what some people in the U.K. today might call, mini celebrities – for example, I remember meeting Stephen Hawking, the Theoretic Physicist and Cosmologist, a few times; he was an Honorary fellow of the college. Then, I think Ken Clark, the Conservative Party politician, and for a brief time, the Chancellor of the Exchequer, was also a student of Gonville & Caius, where he had read for a degree in Law.

But going back to what my degree, I would say the Engineering course at Cambridge University was very tough – very theoretical, and underpinned by a lot of very advanced Maths - where we were challenged to solve a lot of very complex Engineering problems (tested through Example Papers, or P-Sets, as they then used to call them).

Because I did quite well in my first and second years, in my third year, I was selected to go on to attend the competitive exchange program at the Technology Institute, M.I.T. in Boston, the U.S.A. – which was another great experience. After that, I then returned to the U.K. to complete my fourth and final year.

Apart from the lectures and classroom teachings, I found the various lab practicals and company industrial visits, to be very enjoyable and interesting. Academically, overall, I graduated with a 1st class – which is something I was very, very proud of.

So, yes, overall, an all-round great experience!

Professional Services - PwC

Well, Sachin, that sounds really amazing! It certainly sounds like you had an enjoyable time - lots of hard work, lots of study, but also a lot of opportunity to work with some very diverse, smart and driven people. After you graduated, I see your first full time work experiences were with PwC. How did you end up taking your first steps in the working world, with them?

As with anything, mostly from the opportunities I had been looking for at the time. As graduate, I wanted to try and start my career working for a well-known, well-established, global company, somewhere that would provide me with a strong platform and foundation from which I could build on.

I had enjoyed some of my earlier Research & Development experiences during my gap year (after Sixth Form College and before University), and over the summers, when I was at University. I got to work for companies such as: Renishaw, a High Tech Engineering Company, near Bristol, and then after that, the National Broadcasting & Media Company, the BBC, at their Central Communications and Technology department, in London.

On my degree itself, I had also enjoyed some of the “Engineering Consultancy” projects I got to work on. I got to visit various just-in-time factories at companies such as: Coty Lipsticks, IMI Norgren, and Rolls Royce, in the U.K., and then later, with Hitachi GST, in Japan - to help improve different aspects of their Manufacturing activities – such as reducing assembly line changeover times, achieving better assembly line balancing, reducing WIP & Inventory levels, reducing lead times, trying to increase product throughput rates, and so on.

When, towards the end of my degree, the time came to start thinking about what I might want to do next, many of those close to me (my family, close friends and classmates, etc.) had encouraged me to consider applying for a full-time role in the field of “Management Consultancy” – to see if that was something that might interest me. There would be some overlap with the skills I had developed and Engineering experiences I had gained during my degree, but it would also provide me with a newer, and slightly different perspective into business, given my, uptil then, only very Research & Development like experiences. Because I was interested in broadening and deepening my experiences, and also learning more about business, I thought, why not, I’d give it a try?

So, that’s what I had started to focus my job application efforts on. After applying to a few places in my 3rd year, I received an offer to work, in my penultimate summer (the summer before the start of my final year at University), at a company called PwC, in their “Transaction Advisory” department, in a small division called “The Strategy Group”.

I had an enjoyable time – it was quite different to my earlier gap year, and internship experiences, but still interesting work – doing desk-based “Commercial Due Diligence” research on different kinds of companies.

The particular division I worked in, “The Strategy Group”, would often get its work from other outside Investment Companies in London. These companies would often approach our division, because they needed an independent team, such as ours, to carry out some market research on a particular target company they were interested in buying. (e.g. to better understand what products and services the target company sold, what its positioning was vis a vis its closest competitors, what the latest market trends and developments affecting the company were, what customer’s opinions about the company were, and what regulatory developments might affect the company’s future growth).

At the end of that summer, I had been given an offer to work for them full time. Because I didn’t want to start my fourth year at University without a firm job offer in hand, I accepted their offer to work for them when I would graduate. Amongst other things, I felt that having an offer in hand after the end of my 3rd year, would allow me focus more on the course materials of my 4th year. It also allowed me to spend more time planning and preparing for a Manufacturing Engineering Project trip to Japan - where I had gone to work for Hitachi GST for 10 weeks, near Tokyo, on an interesting just-in-time factory automation project. So, I was quite content with all the decisions I had made.

Again, really great to hear Sachin. Just after you graduated, how did you feel before you moved down from Cambridge to London? I imagine you must have been both quite excited, but also a little nervous, about starting your first full time job?

Well, yes, that’s very true. I felt both excited, but also a little nervous and anxious.

Excited because I knew PwC were considered as one of the top graduate employers in the country, quite well-known in many areas of business. I used to often see and hear in the news, news reporters quoting statements from Market Research reports that had been written and published by PwC.

I also knew that PwC were a global company, with a large network of international offices, so it would mean opportunities too of working on projects, with people from very different backgrounds and countries, all very motivated to work and learn, which I also saw as a plus!

The offer too was in London, which I was excited about. The city is considered to be a thriving epicentre for business. With more commerce and companies, there are also more opportunities for career development and progression. Away from business, the city itself is also very diverse and cosmopolitan, with no shortage of things for someone in their mid 20s to do!

But, yes, you’re absolutely right, I was a bit nervous too. But I think this is only natural, and happens whenever someone starts a new job, moves to different city or place, and meets new people for the first time. There are so many new things to find out, there are lots of things you have to learn, very, very quickly. It all takes time to settle in.

So, you started at PwC, working in a division called “The Strategy Group”, in the companies “Transaction Advisory” department, doing mostly “Commercial Due Dilligence” work, for external Investment Companies (PE firms, VC firms, sometimes listed Corporates). Then later, I see you started working towards obtaining a Chartered Qualification in Finance & Accountancy. How did that come about?

Well, yes, that’s exactly right. I started with company’s “The Strategy Group” division (where I had worked at in an earlier summer internship) doing “Commercial Due Diligence” work, for Investment Companies.

But as I meet more people from other departments (those closest to mine were the “Financial Due Diligence”, and “Market Valuation & Strategy” divisions) and learnt more about what they did, that’s when I discovered how quite a lot of them held or were working towards a “Chartered Qualification” – and that many of them were going through an extra structured training course to try and obtain one. So I became curious.

From a long-term career standpoint, I had always felt that getting some kind of Chartered Qualification would stand me in good stead. The CA, the Qualification that PwC encourages many of its employees to do is: a well-respected business qualification; internationally very well recognised; and teaches you some of the key skills needed to run a successful business - in Finance & Accoutancy, Tax, and Business Law. I saw it as an opportunity to learn more about things I never really knew much about and would never otherwise get much of an opportunity to learn.

I didn’t see anything wrong with work I was doing on “Commercial Due Dilligence” assignments. I had become better and better at that for about 18 months. But I felt that my natural interests at that time were starting to gravitate towards solving the kind of complex “Financial” problems that I saw people in the other departments, with CAs, get to work on.

And the more research I did on the Qualification, and of what one would learn, the more and more intrigued I became.So, after doing what I felt was enough research, I decided to ask some of the Partners in my division whether it would be possible for me to pursue a CA with them. Many of the Partners knew how easily bored I would get without a tough challenge on my hands, and what my natural strengths at that time were, so had encouraged me to speak to more people. Some did say that a Chartered Qualification might not be something I might be able to pursue from within their division, because of some of the requirements of the Qualification – that you had to do work that was more closely linked to what “Finance” people did, rather than to what “Commercial Due Dilligence” people did. This made sense!

So, they then introduced me to some of the Partners in the “Financial Due Dilligence” division. Unfortunately, the Partners there said I had just missed out on the deadline for that year’s intake, and that if I wanted to start with them, I would have to wait another year. After that, I got introduced to some Partners in other departments. That’s when, I think, I met a Partner from Australia, who said they still had some room for a few empty places in the company’s “Investment Management Group” division, in their Assurance deparment, and that they would be willing to bring me in.

So, that’s when I took up their offer, and moved divisions, from the company’s “The Strategy Group” division (in their Advisory department) to the company’s “Investment Management Group” division (in their Assurance department).

The work was a little different in the “Investment Management Group” division (less to do with doing commercial market research, but more business legal and accounting investigative, mathematical and financial in nature. In fact, some of the mathematical complexity of the work reminded me of some of problems I got to work on at some of the just-in-time factories I visited during my Engineering degree. My role also involved a lot more travel to different client offices, so I was developing a new skill set.

Apart from the vast amount of knowledge I picked up, amongst the things I did, practically I got to learn how to value different kinds of “Financial Investments”, using different kinds of “Financial Models”. The experience too of working for many different companies gave me a better first hand appreciation of some of the challenges and risks (e.g. financial client exposures, breach of bank covenants) that might be faced when running a business.

It was an exprience that made me realise that Finance is something that sits at the beating heart of any business, and is a very important area to understand and get to grips with. After 3 years, working in the “Investment Management Group”, I successfully got my Chartered Qualification.

I’m so happy to hear that Sachin! I know from a lot of people that the CA can be a very challenging qualification to get. Any Chartered Qualification, in any industry, especially one that takes 3 years, is a challenge! It’s something that needs a lot of dedication, a lot of focus and commitment, not just because of all the tough exams you have to sit – but also because you have to work in a demanding full-time job at the same time! So, congratulations on persisting with that! So, I wonder, after you qualified, and got your Chartered Qualification, what did you decide to do next?

Well, the day I qualified and got my CA, I went out and celebrated with friends, of course, who went through the same training as me. I remember going out and buying myself a very expensive bottle of Champagne - (a Dom Perignon, I distinctly remember)! That was a great feeling!

In terms of actual work, I wasn’t entirely sure about what I wanted to do next. Quite a few people in my peer group, moved across to another division or department, with the company. Some left the company to pursue their own thing. My bosses in “The Investment Management Group” were quite pleased with the progress I had been making, and work I was doing. I think they also saw me as a very valuable team member that was giving them good continuity of work to my then main client – “The Wellcome Trust” – the London based, Medical Research Charity.

Personally, I also didn’t feel like I needed to move anywhere else immediately. I had established a good relationship with the various people at my main client, “The Wellcome Trust”. I also felt like I was doing good work, and still learning new things, as I was able to further develop my management, supervisory, coaching and mentoring skills, as for example, other, less experienced members joined my team. So, I decided to stay, with my division for another 2 years – and ended up working with the “Investment Management Group”, in London, for a full 5 years!

It was a pretty amazing extra 2 years too! I continued working for my main client, “The Wellcome Trust”, doing finance type of work (e.g. in financial modelling, valuations, negotiations, assurance, advisory, etc.) - either doing myself, or checking the work of others - but I was also able to work for a variety of other companies too! For example, for an Engineering Construction Company (Balfour Beauty) – on a Renewable Energy Fund they were looking to set up; for the Pharmaceutical Company (Johnson & Johnson) to help their Business Assurance team with some year end audit checks; for two Commercial Real Estate Companies (Land Securities, and Deutsche Bank RREEF); And then for a short period of time, for the firms “Sustainability & Climate Change Group” division – working on a project for the IMF (International Monetary Fund), to help them create a new Climate Investment Fund to invest in new Renewable Energy projects – something I was proud to be a part of… thinking about the work my Grandfather did in designing and building Hydroelectric Dams, in India.

Well Sachin, it certainly sounds like you got involved in plenty of projects and areas, working for companies across quite a few sectors. When you were there, working towards both your Qualification AND for the company’s Clients, how was juggling that? What would you now say to other people, perhaps new graduates or other young professionals, who might be aspiring to follow a very similar path, early on in their career?

Well, I would say, as with any path, there can be both peaks and troughs, and I found that to be the case with my experiences.

Before I started at PwC, some people would warn me of the sometimes very long hours I would have to work, both in the company’s “Advisory” department, but also in the “Assurance” department in their “Investment Management Group” division. They also said that the CA, at a Professional Services firm, was also no easy thing to get! They warned me of how hard and challenging it would be!

Now, I will admit, the hours were long! That I can say! But I think, the hours were probably just as long in the company’s other divisions too. I also think that when you’re starting something new, whatever company or industry that may be in, and achieving a “high standard” is your goal, then when do you not have to put in some long hours?

Initially, when I started, I did sometimes find working as the most “junior” person in the team as quite frustrating. You can end up being given a lot of mundane work, and you might not get to participate in as many client meetings, as you perhaps might like to, compared to those more senior to you. But, again, I think, the same is probably true at a lot of places, and depends heavily on what knowledge and skills, experitise and experiences you already have, and what you can realistically bring to the table!

Overall, when I look back at those years, I did feel like I was able to learn a lot, both from the formal training I went through, but also some of the people I worked with – motivated, smart, very hard working people – some of whom I am still in quite close touch with today!

In these kinds of companies, it is true, you can end up working with people – managers, partners - who can be really difficult to deal with. But I think, it is important to not let that affect your work, or for you to be affected by that kind of environment!

One thing, I would say, that I did not like, was that I had a lot of outside interests and hobbies: Tennis, Cricket, wathcin Formula 1, Films & Music, getting into Health & Fitness, and Cooking, amongst others. I also wanted to spend more time socialising with friends, outside of work, and to visit more places, socially, in London.

But because of the long hours, I personally found these difficult to fit in. The volumes of work can be very high, and there are always deadlines to meet. And trying to do this, whilst doing all the extra work needed, for 3 years, to complete my C.A., wasn’t easy! It wasn’t impossible, but it was a lot of hours and a lot of hard work, and looking back I had to sacrifice quite a lot in terms of my social life and social activities, (e.g. when I worked across a lot of weekends -for 3 years – which was tough)!

What I would say to others that might be considering following a similar path?

I think it really depends on what you want from the experience, and how you think it will help with your long-term career plan. I think many people’s career plans do change and shift overtime – as their interests change and shift over time - it’s only natural!

I think what people should ask themselves is what avenues will open up or be available to them after they get enough of the kinds of experience they want, or level of standard in their work they are looking to achieve, before they decide to move on, however many years that might be - that’s of course if they don’t decide to stay where they are forever.

Understanding what experiences, knowledge and skills you get at each stage and how that fits into your Bigger Career Picture will definitely help you become more committed and convinced to any decisions you do end up making. This will help you to move forward with a stronger sense of purpose and conviction (even if the early stages - say first 2 years somewhere are very challening because everything is so new to you). And if you’re unsure, then get some advice beforehand - from friends, career coaches, mentors, potential sponsors, or other people close to you that you trust.

When I look at my own experiences: - towards the end of my University degree, after getting encouragement from family and friends to try something with a company that might give me a slightly different perspective into ‘Business’ from the “Research & Development” experiences I had gained till then, I decided to start by working some years at a Professional Services company, to learn more and get some experience in areas such as Management Consulting, Finance, Accounting, and Tax

The offer from PwC was a chance to work for a well-known, well-established company, one large enough that I would have the opportunity to work with people from a wide variety of backgrounds - e.g. different nationalities, different university degrees, different ethnicities, and so on - as we worked together to solve tough and challenging, what turned out to be financial and strategic business problems.

And in the 5- 6 years I was there, I felt I had achieved quite a lot:

  • I got an interesting mixture of experiences in from the company’s “Assurance” and “Advisory” departments.
  • I left with a Chartered Qualification, a C.A. – a well-respected, internationally well-recognised, business qualification, something I worked hard to get, and which gave me some of the skills (in Finance, Tax, and Accountancy) I knew I would need to have to successfull work in a business.
  • I felt fortunate to have worked for a wide range of companies too (small, medium sized, and large; private and public; even FTSE 100 companies), across a lot of industries: (media & entertainment, engineering, medical, charitable, educational, real estate, investment management, and so on.)

I don’t think I would have gotten the opportunity to work for such a range of companies and industries had I perhaps worked elsewhere.

  • It felt too, from the department I mostly worked in, that I got a better appreciation of the different kinds of risks that might be faced when running a business (e.g. knowing what “Accounts Debtors” meant, and how important when trying to minimise your Client Exposures)

I think it has definitely helped me towards achieving my long term career plan – of “one day” being someone who can works effectively with other people to solve ‘globally important and challenging problems; to support creative, talented, and innovative people, in the ‘Media, Entertainment”, and “Technology & Engineering*” sectors - as a Senior Business Leader‘

– It’s what I have over time discovered my passion to be!

But going back to answering the original question - what I would say to others considering pursuing a similar path?:

Well, in Professional Services, at least in the department I was in, the hours can be both long and intense. And in the early years (not months, but first 2 to 3 years), you will likely have to sacrifice quite a lot in terms of the other extra-curricular activities you might want to pursue. For me, the workloads definitely felt more than how I worked during my University Degree, from the teams and people I worked in and with.

That said, hard work (provided you take care of your health and mental well-being) usually does eventually pay off. You will definitely learn about Business (some functions), and you will definitely, if you decide to pursue something like a C.A. Qualification, get to work on solving some pretty “interesting” and “complex” mathematical problems, and learn more about what “Finance & Accountancy” and “Tax” is. So, it is something that will definitely provide you with a very strong foundation to build on, whatever the next stages of you career.

This of course is just the first stage that I happened to choose, from the different opportunities that came to me at different moments in time, and encouragement I got from those around me.

Other people, in their own minds, might have a slightly different path in mind. Some people, for example, may already have in their minds a very strong sense of what their passion is, and what they want their “sector focus” to be, and consequently just work for a company in that sector, maybe the by pursuing a CIMA, (a different type of Finance & Accounting qualitication). Then, once they are in that sector, they know they want to be strongly affiliated with I guess then decide on what they want their “role focus” to be. After that they will then try to start creating the right opportunities for themselves.

Business School - MBA

Thank you for sharing that Sachin! I’m sure some of our readers will really appreciate you taking the time to share your experiences in this way – I personally, don’t think very many people, from the outset, ever really figure out their entire career path out completely - sector or role focus, or ever try to be too savvy about following a career path in exactly the way they might have originally imagined. But everyone eventually does find their own path, and true compass.

So, tell me more. As you were working at PwC, why after 6 years did you decide to leave your role, to pursue your MBA?

Really, the honest answer is that I just saw it as another challenge. Getting a Chartered Qualification was a challenge, I found my work, initially, in PwC’s “Advisory” department also quite challenging. I’m think I’m just the combined product of all my past experiences.

Was it Chuck Palanuick who said, “Nothing of me is original. I am just the combined effort of everything and everybody I have ever known”

I think there is a lot of truth to this saying. Over the years, I think I have just been extremely lucky to have worked with friends, work colleagues, career coaches, and mentors who have just instilled in me some great character.

And this is just huge luck of the people I’ve had the opportunity to work with – and why I still strongly believe that meeting new people and opening yourself out to new and different experiences, is critically important for ones own personal development - and something I am always pro-actively seeking to do!

What is your definition of wealth and a successful life?

I think there is no clear definition, because it means different things to different people.

  • For some people, financial wealth is very important, not necessarily because they are materialistic, but because it is something their family never had – and this is one way they can achieve that level of comfort - or psychological peace of mind.
  • For other people, it might be success with their career
  • For some it might be family with other people.
  • For others, it might be health and fitness.

For me personally, after living in two very small rooms in London, I think my definition of wealth and a successful life, would be some combination of all of the above! You know,… the usual of what men seem to want these days, a large room, with a desk(!), a (solar powered) house, a nice modern (hybrid-electric) sports car, a nice kitchen, some gadgets and electronics, some financial security, etc.

I think, if there was to be a very strict definition of wealth and a successful life – it would be an intangible and general one – to just be happy and fulfilled, in whatever it is you do or want to do! But that of course, will mean different things to different people!

If you wake up and feel, “I’m grateful to have this day”, then that is a sign of real success! But if you wake up and think, “I just need to get through this day”, then that is a sign you might want to do something different!

Thanks Sachin, that was really thoughtfully answered! Now, through your journey as a business person, can you tell us something that you’ve learnt, just about life, not necessarily about business, but just about life, and people. Is there any bit of advice in general, or thing(s) that you’ve learnt, that you’d like to pass on to our readers?

Yes, it’s a very, very small world, and you should never – you must never – burn your bridges, because it will always come back round to you in the end, at some point in time, or through some channel! All of your actions, and all of your reactions, and the way you treat people, will always come back to you!

You know, that kind of golden rule, “do onto others as you’d like done onto you”. That sort of thing, is something that really resonates with me, and I think, strongly in business too.

Integrity is something you cannot regain!

I’ve seen time and time again, people burn their bridges, push themselves into a corner, and overreact, and overreact badly! (I’ve been guilty of it myself sometimes). But it does no one any favours! So, my one piece of advice would be just that.

Then, my second piece of advice would be to just be true to yourself. Work out what it is that inspires you and gets you out of bed in the morning. What are the one or two things that you really feel passionate about? Then put 110% of yourself behind achieving those things! I know it’s very general, but that’s the best way to achieve your goals.

One last question, and I promise this will be the last. Why did you choose the name Star Tech Enterprises for the name of your freelance company? That’s quite an interesting name!

Well, I wanted to go onto a mission to “support businesses around the world – anywhere from large multi-nationals, medium-sized companies, to small and innovative start-ups, and help them achieve more! – particularly those in the ‘Technology & Engineering’, and ‘Media & Entertainment’ sectors”.

And so, I wanted the name of the company to reflect this in some way. I chose the word “Star”, as I saw that as something that indicated excellence – just like what you see in the night sky, I wanted my company to work with an ambition and standard that was “hotly plasmonic”! Every individual, if they truly love their work, should “reach for the Stars!”

Then I chose the word “Tech” because, personally, I believe in the continued application of both Science and Technology as requisites to a healthy and modern society – in making the systems and cultures we live in, more predictable, more effective, and more enjoyable, so that we may all live happier and healthier lives.

Then I chose the word “Enterprises”, because, just like the great explorers of “The Starship Enterprise” from the T.V. series Star Trek (directed by J.J. Abrams), I wanted the company’s name to reflect the same values of its daring Crew of voyagers - that is: “to seek out new discoveries” and “to boldly go where no man or woman has gone before” – always with an “adventurous spirit”, and always with a “high sense of courage, boldness, and ingenuity!”.

Hence, when combined together, you get the name “Star-Tech Enterprises”
