Supercars!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 02/04/2017

Some interesting views on Supercars from this month’s Top Gear magazine…

“If there is nothing else that one should learn from supreme performance in motor sports (or any high performance vehicle in the automotive industry, for that matter), it is the importance of the basics of: rigidity, inertia, and light weight.

The fact that many F1 cars still obliterate “supercars” is down to the fact that F1’s quest for light weight has been rigorously followed!

There is no time, when a car is in motion, where weight doesn’t impair performance, be it cornering, changing direction, accelerating, braking… something that is surely understood by most “supercar” designers. However, what is often forgotten is that power to weight and weight to power ratios are two completely different things.

It’s seems that the current crop of supercars are endlessly chasing performance figures – top speed, or acceleration, over everything else. But, really, not many owners (yes super car owners) will ever experience the top speed of their supercar, and the difference between 3.0 seconds, and 3.2 seconds in driving 0-60mph is impossible to notice.

So what’s more important? Well, the overall driving experience - which means, control feedback and engine noise. These will contribute much more to satisfying a customer’s need for speed than a fraction of a second improvement in acceleration times.

Do I sympathize with supercar designers? Yes, absolutely!

One nightmare for them is the vicious circle that leads to a “waterfall of weight increases”.

How?

There is a trend in most current supercars moving towards turbo charged engines in the race for power and speed – with a resultant turbo lag and uninspiring engine noise. Moreover, although the move to battery assistance fills the turbo lag, it leads to a massive increase in powertrain weight – and necessitates and increase in size in the cooling pack (something else to cool!), and increases in the load on the suspension, chassis, and brakes – all with undesirable consequences on the vehicles dynamic performance.

Just look at the race to the top-speed chase. It took the Bugatti Veron an extra 750kg and 565bhp to better an F1’s top speed - the “pure” driving experience has been left behind as an ever increasing number of electronic aids – needed to control the huge amounts of power and torque – have dumbed the feedback.

There is no question that performance figures are important for selling supercars, but maybe the focus should move from power to weight, in the journey needed to achieve these figures - that is rigidity, inertia, and lightweight!

So where does this leave the future of supercars? Well, we still need them (I think we do)! They are fascinating and exciting! They are needed for inspiration and to experiment with new materials and technologies, and even architectures, all of which have and can continue to result in important advances in high volume cars.

Car enthusiasts can still have an affair with the breed, but perhaps we all need a good and proper reminding of the fact that the “laws of physics” still don’t change!”
