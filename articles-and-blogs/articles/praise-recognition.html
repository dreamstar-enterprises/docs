Providing Praise and Recognition

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 13/12/2015

Providing praise and recognition is an important managerial and leadership practice. It means showing appreciation to others for outstanding performance, significant achievements, and important contributions.

But most recognition, even praise, is under-utilized by many people in the workplace, with their subordinates, seniors and colleagues. This begs the question, what should you recognize, when should you give it, who should you recognize, and what form of recognition should you use?

Here are a few lessons from my learnings, readings and experiences!

Recognise a variety of contributions and achievements

The biggest problem is that many people aren’t able to identify or articulate their achievements or contributions. (So accordingly they need to take the time to identify and articulate these).

Achievements can include a variety of things such as:

  1. Demonstrating initiative - and knowing what this means
  2. Putting in extra effort to carry out an assignment or task. How much extra effort?
  3. Achieving very challenging performance goals and standards. How was performance measured? Was this on multiple dimensions? On which aspect did you excel in, which records did you break?
  4. Making personal sacrifices to accomplish a task or objective - in lieu of what else? What have you given up? How important was it? What was the opportunity cost?
  5. Giving helpful suggestions and innovative ideas for improving productivity, or the quality of work - and proving that they worked
  6. Making a special effort to help someone else (a co-worker, a customer) to deal with a problem. How severe was the problem?

As a personal anecdote, when I found out that recently appointed head of Goldman Sachs’ Investment Banking Division (Karen Cook) was an alumni from our business school, I took the initiative to inform our careers and alumni office of this great achievement.

The office were unaware of the achievement, and so subsequently arranged for an official reward ceremony to congratulate and recognize Karen’s achievements, setting the foundsations for a new and prosperous long-term relationship - (and it all began with a little initiative!)

Actively search for contributions to recognize (so you can widen your lens!)

Look for examples of effective behavior to recognize each day. Many people sometimes are so concerned with their own busy day to day lives, that they fail to notice and (constructively) criticize ineffective behavior by subordinates or peers. They also fail to notice and praise effective behavior.

Recognize improvements in performance - and really do!

Some people believe that it is inappropriate to recognize performance improvements if an individuals performance is substandard.

This couldn’t be further from the truth! Recognition for improvements is still important – it encourages and strengthens efforts towards further improvement.

It also communicates “an expectation” towards excellence which can be particularly important for those learning new tasks, or who may not have enough self-confidence.

Recognise commendable efforts that failed - not just the successes!

There is a fallacy that recognition should be limited to successful efforts. For example, when you have to accept that a project will fail rather than allowing it to pro-long at great cost. If this happens, you should thank the person who came up with the idea and explain why the idea could not be implemented. Why? It encourages further suggestions in the future, and doesn’t discourage that person with coming up with more ideas!

Do not limit recognition to just high visibility jobs

It can be a common tendency to provide recognition to individuals whose performance and achievements are highly visible (It’s a natural human emotional tendency and response).

However, the contribution and achievements of all employees should be recognized (even those you don’t always see) - in line functions and those in support functions. Everyone’s contribution counts!

Don’t limit recognition to just the few best performers.

It can be tempting to just award the highest achievers. Instead, it is better to give an award to everyone who exceeds a challenging performance standard.

Extreme competition can have undesirable effects – an unwillingness to help competitors (they may be your competitors now, they may be your partners later), and resentment by people who perform exceptionally well (by most people’s standards) yet receive little or no recognition because somebody else did a little better, often, as it turns out, due to just luxk.

Use appropriate recognition

This will depend on the norms and culture of the organization, but in all cases should be sincere. Overusing a particular form of recognition is not desirable as its effects become diminished as it becomes more commonplace.
