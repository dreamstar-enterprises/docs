Sometimes things need to be believed first, before they can be seen!

Articles & Blogs • Main Category • 11/04/2019

In his book on “Blizscaling” – the lightning fast path to building a massively valuable company, Reid Hoffman gives some very compelling reasons for why and how a Blitzscaling strategy might work for your company.

Here are some bulleted learnings from the first two chapters - and, though I don’t necessarily agree with everything written - I’m of the “Elon Musk” philosophy that a “rising tide floats all boats”, and that growth should not always be about just “winning market share” but also about “legitimising the technology and idea first” - the book is a very motivational read. I hope others can also go out and buy a copy and be inspired by it!

Some of the important lessons so far:

Be Unique and Valuable at the same time!

  • If you’re a founder / entrepreneur, you shouldn’t be pushed into a “legal extortion racket” – instead, push for a “sincere attempt” to want to create true value in a market!

  • The best product (or combined service) almost always wins!

  • Don’t be a mercenary – don’t be misguided into creating something you don’t really want or truly believe in. Instead, be a missionary, and do so with tremendous grit!

  • Take risks, and be willing to risk your reputation and self-image to achieve more, try more, and learn more. Those who attempt to do so, and extoll the virtues of disruption are, coincidentally, the ones more likely to end up in “winner’s circle”!

  • Instead of feeling like the future is being forced upon you, try and be part of building the it!

A Startling Growth Fact!

  • The global economy will need to create six hundred million jobs by 2030 to meet the United Nation’s sustainable development goals

  • This means that the world needs more than just new companies – it needs entire new industries.

Why Think Millions? Think Billions!

(In other words, don’t let your boss, or anyone else stop you from thinking ambitiously)!

This applies to:

  • Entrepreneurs who want to build massive companies,

  • Venture capitalists who want to invest in them

  • Employees who want to work for them, to

  • Governments and forward-looking communities who want to encourage the growth of these companies in their regions!

  • The same lessons apply to non-profits wanting to serve the underprivileged by overturning conventional wisdom and wanting to achieve massive results

Every companies Growth Story is Unique!

  • Regardless of how and what the popular press portrays, the truth is that the formula for building a great company is unique – and depends on the market opportunity, the founders, and the networks in which they operate.

  • That said, though there may be no “one-size” fits all or “must-follow” rule book for everyone – there are patterns, frameworks, and strategies that business goers and entrepreneurs can adapt to their own needs and circumstances

  • Don’t forget that culture matters too!: finance-centric and metric driven companies don’t always work well with design-driven ones! (Think WETA Studios, New Zealand, working with, United Kingdom’s efficiency driven Tescos - they’re two very different companies!)

It’s more than just about technology

  • Technological innovation alone doesn’t make for a thriving company – nor does the combination of venture capital, research universities, or smart people

  • After any mythical moment of creation in the garage (or “shoebox room”), comes the truly hard work knowing how to make it affordable, building the factory, and then hiring people!

Bliztscaling is not for the conventional!

  • Whilst classic business strategy tends to involve gathering information and making decisions when one can be reasonable confident with its results, implicitly prioritizing correctness over efficiency, this approach falls apart when new technologies enable a new market and scramble an existing one

  • Traditional MBA approaches might focus on techniques such as DCFs to make financial decisions with greater certainty. Factory techniques might focus on maximising throughput of an assembly line. But both of these emphasize “efficiency” and “certainty”

  • Whilst, “efficiency” and “certainty” are important in the context of stable, established markets, they offer little guidance to the “disrupters”, “inventors” and the “innovators”. When a market is up for grabs, the risk usually isn’t efficiency. Why? Because if you win, efficiency isn’t that important! If you lose, efficiency is completely irrelevant!

What being able to Bliztscale means:

  • Blitzscaling means having the skill and courage (self-belief, innate ability, resources, and clear goals) to go ahead, plan and execute with 110% effort!

  • It also means needing an environment that is willing to finance intelligent risks (those one can measure and afford) – with both financial and human capital (i.e. people and money) as the driving ingredients.

Software will usually always play a pivotal role!

  • Computer software offers nearly unlimited scalability, in terms of storage and distribution!

  • Now, even software enabled hardware (think Fitbit, or the Tesla car), allows for innovation on software timescales (within days and weeks rather than years)!

  • Industries that were just focused on physical products now need to be well integrated through software to innovate and survive. Examples of great integrations will include:

    • Precision medicine that will revolutionize healthcare

    • Smart Grids that will dramatically improve power efficiency and enable the spread of renewable energy sources

    • Computational biology that will help improve life itself!

The Certainty/Uncertainty; Speed/Efficiency framework

Bliztscaling isn’t for everyone, so it’s important to assess what kind of enterprise you are, and where you are at!

Classic Start-Up Growth:

  • This stage prioritizes “efficiency” in the face of “uncertainty”, like “jumping off a cliff and assembling an airplane on the way down!” It’s a good strategy to follow when you are trying to establish certainty, and product / market fit!

Classic Scale-Up Growth

  • Most mature companies focus on growing “efficiency” once they have achieved a certain level of “certainty” about their environment. Here the measure of success are “hurdle / waterfall” rates, so that “ROIs” exceed the “costs of capital”. This is a good strategy to follow when you’re trying to maximize returns in an established market - in say a large corporate.

Fastscaling

  • Here you’re willing to sacrifice some efficiency for “speed”! But because you’re already in an environment of “certainty”, the costs are well understood and predictable. This is a good strategy to follow when trying to gain market share and hit revenue milestones. This is very popular with analysts and bankers, who can feel confident that they can then create financial models that can work out to the penny ROI of a fast scaling investment

Bliztscaling!

  • This is when you’re willing to “sacrifice efficiency” for “speed”, without waiting to achieve certainty on whether the sacrifice will pay off – just as if you were “assembling a fast plane, strapping and on and igniting a set of jet engines (and possibly some afterburners too!) – while you still build the wings!”

  • Blitscaling is much tougher to implement, unless you’re a Microsoft or Google, with deep pockets.

  • It is harder to raise money too from investors for a calculated gamble (blitzscaling) than it is for a sure thing (fastscaling), because with blitscaling you have to keep more capital in reserve to recover from the mistakes you’re likely to make on the way.
