    Possible improvements to Payroll in Film & H.E.T.V.

    Market Research • SL • 07/03/2022

    Better Payroll Solution for U.K. Film & H.E.T.V.

    ×

    Overview

    Financial Management (including Payroll) in Film & H.E.T.V. is very different to Financial Management in traditional companies: Volumes of paperwork processed are higher; Processes are more time critical (approvals need to get done weekly rather than monthly); The workforce being managed is more varied (freelances, self-employed workers, and employees); Timesheet systems are more complex (in T.V. & Drama) over 8 different types of overtime, with workers paid by the hour (i.e. extra for every extra hour they work)

    Although, when I worked a H.E.T.V. production last year - our team did manage to pay all 850+ Crew workers, weekly, it often meant long hours, and a high workload, perhaps excessively so.

    Despite its size and some areas of complexity, I do still think there is plenty of potential to modernise certain aspects of how work is done - especially for processes related to payroll, e.g.: with on-boarding, timesheets, and payments - that can be so high volume, and so time critical.

    I have highlighted some of the challenges and problems I faced (or rather our team faced) on our H.E.T.V. production below. (Perhaps, you might relate to some of them, on your own productions…)

    • Workflows were not as well connected as they could have been. Documents, though received digitally, got approved in large batches, (usually a 200pg single Adobe.pdf with a mixture of, for example, partially signed or not signed timesheets, invoices, and other claims forms). This came with all the inherent problems of processing items in batches (where a single rejected invoice that needed re-approval could hold up an entire batch).

    • Pay Documents (Payslips, Timesheets, Invoices, etc.) were filed and archived from disparate systems. Filing, particulary, was very time-consuming, and became even more of a problem, for example, when Timesheets didn’t get signed on time (since already filed documents had to be located, re-opened, and then updated. This was so any partially signed Timesheets could get replaced by their fully signed versions, when those did eventually get signed). Filing alone took upto 20% of our time (during Shoot) - about 1 day out of a 5 day week. Close to 80% to 100% of our time during Wrap!

    • Embedded documents (within digital envelopes) didn’t always get opened and reviewed. This is partly a volume issue because of the very high volumes of Crew workers we were processing a week (850+ at times). This resulted in workers sometimes getting paid as if they were a Limited Company, when they should have been paid as Self-Employed, or as an Employee, or vice versa - which later became a headache to correct with the Payroll Company. (It’s nicer to get things pre-validated up front, and right first time!)

    • Upgrades (informing of increases in Day Rates to particular workers, for example, or other changes in Contract terms, such as changes to weekly allowances) were often communicated late from the Production Office - and this was often only because they were told late by the respective Head of Departments. Timesheets were also often signed late (this was quite a big issue), and needed changing, requiring the need to make many retrospective changes (sometimes going back 2-3 months) to Contracts that had already been signed and issued, or Timesheets that had already been paid.

    • V.A.T. Invoices were a challenge to keep track of, as these were often sent late, or sometimes lost through daily email Inboxes, or sometimes never sent - so a lot of chasing had to be done. I can’t say with real confidence that every person on that production, got paid the correct amount of V.A.T. - despite our best efforts. Without a proper automated Tracking system, this will always be quite a challenge to keep on top of (similar to how there is no automated Tracking system for calculating the cumulative Holiday Pay of Crew Members - one not based on a weekly %, but on time elapsed since the Contract Start)

    • Small subsequent changes to contracts (e.g. a changes in Vehicle allowances, or Computer allowances, or Day Rates), often meant having to re-open a contract, and re-setting the entire approval chain. This had knock on effect to any Timesheets that had, or hadn’t yet been paid. A system of Fast Track approvals chains for ‘small changes’, that would have expedited this process, did not exist.

    • Final Payroll Journals from the Payroll Company, were not always that neat. ‘Account Code’ changes had to be carefully checked. Step-up (upgrade) days, and changes to a workers job roles, or department, or any extra hours worked in past weeks (that had not yet been paid), had to be carefully and manually split out into separate lines. Payroll journals often had blanks and coding errors in them, that had to be corrected weekly.

    • Incorrect bank details often needed to be chased at the end of the week, which could get quite frantic, and sometimes hold up the entire payroll run.

    • System generated Timesheets were not always correct, and had to be digitally annotated (by drawing a red boxes or notes, on each incorrect timesheet PDF file manually, through Adobe), to correct any figures. Overtimes and amounts in the Timesheet system we used couldn’t be overridden directly (within the software system).

    With all the above problems and challenges experienced first hand, I decided to spend some time thinking about some possible solutions to these problems - and how it might be possible, with some effort, to design a better system that could overcome them.
