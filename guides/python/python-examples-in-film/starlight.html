    Possible improvements to Payroll in Film & H.E.T.V.

    Market Research • SL • 07/03/2022

    Better Payroll Solution for U.K. Film & H.E.T.V.

    ×

    Overview

    Financial Management (including Payroll) in Film & H.E.T.V. productions is very different to Financial Management in traditional companies or corporates: Volumes of paperwork processed are much higher; Processes are more time critical (approvals need to get done weekly rather than monthly); The make-up of the workforce is also more varied (freelances, self-employed workers, and employees); Timesheet systems are very complex, with for example, over 8 different types of overtime, with workers paid by the hour.

    Although, when I worked on an Apple (Original Production), H.E.T.V. production - (directed by Tom Hanks and Stephen Spielberg) - our team did manage to pay all 850+ Crew workers by the end of the week, it was a long and arduous hours, and there is, I strongly feel, still plenty of scope and potential to improve and modernise the way we did and approached our work - especially for a process (on-boarding, timesheets, and payments) that was so high volume and so time critical!

    I have highlighted some of the challenges and problems we faced in the Finance department, on our Apple Production, below (perhaps, you might relate to some of them…):

    • Workflows were not always well connected. Documents, though received digitally, got approved in large batches, (usually a 200pg single Adobe.pdf with a mixture of partially signed or not signed timesheets, invoices, and other claims forms). This came with all the inherent problems of processing items in batches (a single rejected invoice that needed re-approval would hold up an entire workflow).

    • Pay Documents (Payslips, Timesheets, Invoices, etc.) are filed and archived from disparate systems. Filing, particulary, was very time-consuming, and became even more of a problem when, for example, Timesheets didn’t get signed on time (since already filed documents had to be re-opened, and updated so any partially signed Timesheets could get replayed by their fully signed versions when those did eventually get signed). Filing alone took upto 20% of our time (during Shoot). More during Wrap!

    • Embedded documents (within digital envelopes) didn’t always get opened and reviewed (partly because of the very high volumes of Crew workers). This resulted in people sometimes getting paid as if they were a Limited Company, when they should have been paid as Self-Employed, or as an Employee, or vice versa - something that became a headache to correct with the Payroll Company.

    • Upgrades (informing of increases in Day Rates to particular Crew Members, for example, or other changes in Contract terms, such as weekly allowances) were often communicated late from the Production Office. Timesheets were also often signed late, and needed changing, requiring the need to make many retrospective changes (sometimes going back 2-3 months) to Contracts that had already been signed and issued, or Timesheets that had already been paid,

    • V.A.T. Invoices were a challenge to keep track of, as these were often sent late, or sometimes lost through daily email Inboxes, or sometimes never sent - so a lot of chasing had to be done. I can’t say with real confidence that every person on that production, got paid the correct amount of V.A.T. - despite our efforts

    • Small subsequent changes to contracts (e.g. a changes in Vehicle allowances, or Computer allowances, or Day Rates), often meant having to re-open a contract and resetting the entire approval chain, with the knock on effect to any Timesheets that had or hadn’t yet been paid. A system of Fast Track approvals chains for ‘small changes’, that would have expedited this process, did not exist.

    • Final Payroll Journals from the Payroll Company, were not always that neat. ‘Account Code’ changes had to be carefully checked. Step-up (upgrade) days, and changes in roles, and any extra hours worked in past weeks that had been worked but not paid, had to be appropriately split out into separate lines, to be correctly recorded. Payroll journals often had blanks and coding errors in them, that had to be corrected weekly!

    • Incorrect bank details often needed to be chased at the end of the week, which could get quite frantic, and sometimes hold up the entire payroll run.

    • System generated Timesheets were not always correct, and had to be digitally annotated (by drawing a red box on each incorrect timesheet PDF file manually, through Adobe), to correct any figures. Overtimes and amounts in the Timesheet system we used couldn’t be overridden directly.

    With all the above problems and challenges experienced first hand, I therefore spent some time thinking about what the possible solutions to these problems could be - and how it might be possible, with some effort, to design a better system that could overcome each of these problems.
